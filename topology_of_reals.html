Definitions

A neighborhood centered on \(p\) with radius \(\varepsilon \gt 0\) is the set of all points that are within distance \(\varepsilon\) of \(p\). $$N(p,\varepsilon) = \lbrace x : d(x,p) \lt \varepsilon \rbrace$$

Let \(A\) be a subset of \(\mathbb{R}\). \(p \in \mathbb{R}\) is an interior point of \(A\) iff there exists a neighborhood around \(p\) contained inside \(A\). $$p \text{ is an interior point of } A \iff \exists \varepsilon \gt 0, N(p,\varepsilon) \subset A$$

Let \(A\) be a subset of \(\mathbb{R}\). The interior of \(A\) is the set of all interior points of \(A\), and is denoted \(\operatorname{int} A\) $$\operatorname{int} A = \lbrace p : p \text{ is an interior point of } A \rbrace$$

Let \(A\) be a subset of \(\mathbb{R}\). \(p \in \mathbb{R}\) is an exterior point of \(A\) iff there exists a neighborhood around \(p\) countained outside \(A\). $$p \text{ is an exterior point of } A \iff \exists \varepsilon \gt 0, N(p,\varepsilon) \subset A^c$$

Let \(A\) be a subset of \(\mathbb{R}\). The exterior of \(A\) is the set of all exterior points of \(A\), and is denoted \(\operatorname{ext} A\) $$\operatorname{ext} A = \lbrace p : p \text{ is an exterior point of } A \rbrace$$

Let \(A\) be a subset of \(\mathbb{R}\). \(p \in \mathbb{R}\) is a boundary point of \(A\) iff every neighborhood around \(p\) contains points inside of \(A\) and outside of \(A\). $$\forall \varepsilon \gt 0, N(p,\varepsilon) \cap A \neq \emptyset \text{ and } N(p,\varepsilon) \cap A^c \neq \emptyset$$

Let \(A\) be a subset of \(\mathbb{R}\). The boundary of \(A\) is the set of all boundary points of \(A\), and is denoted \(\partial A\) $$\partial A = \lbrace p : p \text{ is an boundary point of } A \rbrace$$

Let \(A\) be a subset of \(\mathbb{R}\). \(p \in \mathbb{R}\) is an adherent point of \(A\) iff every neighborhood around \(p\) contains a point in \(A\). $$p \text{ is an adherent point of } A \iff \forall \varepsilon \gt 0, N(p,\varepsilon) \cap A \neq \emptyset$$

Let \(A\) be a subset of \(\mathbb{R}\). \(p \in \mathbb{R}\) is an accumulation point of \(A\) iff every neighborhood around \(p\) contains a point in \(A\) that are not \(p\). $$p \text{ is an accumulation point of } A \iff \forall \varepsilon \gt 0, (N(p,\varepsilon) \setminus \lbrace p \rbrace) \cap A \neq \emptyset$$

Let \(A\) be a subset of \(\mathbb{R}\). \(p \in \mathbb{R}\) is an isolated point of \(A\) iff \(p \in A\) and there exists a neighborhood around \(p\) with no other point in \(A\). The second condition is equivilant to saying \(p\) is not an accumulation point $$p \text{ is an isolated point of } A \iff \exists \varepsilon \gt 0, (N(p,\varepsilon) \setminus \lbrace p \rbrace) \cap A = \emptyset \iff p \in A \text{ and } p \text{ is not an accumulation point}$$

A set of real numbers \(A\) is open iff all the points in \(A\) are interior points of \(A\) iff \(A\) does not contain its boundaries. $$A \text{ is open} \iff A = \operatorname{int} A \iff A \cap \partial A = \emptyset$$

Proof \(A = \operatorname{int} A \iff A \cap \partial A = \emptyset\).

Let \(A = \operatorname{int} A\). Any \(p \in A\) has a neighborhood contained in \(A\) with no members in \(A^c\). This means that no \(p \in A\) is a boundary point, so \(A \cap \partial A = \emptyset\).

Let \(A \cap \partial A = \emptyset\).
